The Origin of the Turkish Language

Historical Background

The Turkish language is a member of the Turkic language family, which belongs to the Altaic language group. Its origins trace back to the Central Asian steppes, around 5000–8000 years ago.

First Speakers and Their Lives

The earliest speakers of proto-Turkic languages were nomadic tribes in Central Asia. These tribes lived in areas around the Altai Mountains and Siberia. Their lifestyle was primarily nomadic, involving herding, hunting, and trading. They were known for their use of horses and their expertise in metallurgy, particularly in crafting weapons and tools. These people had strong oral traditions, with early Turkic inscriptions, such as the Orkhon inscriptions (8th century), marking some of the oldest written records.

Language, Science, and Innovation

The Science of Language

Contributions to Science and Innovation The Turkish language and scholars have contributed to various fields of science: Ulugh Beg: A 15th-century Turkic astronomer and mathematician. Taqi al-Din: An Ottoman-era polymath who made significant advancements in astronomy and engineering.

Language Innovations

The language underwent a script reform in 1928, transitioning from the Arabic script to the Latin alphabet under Mustafa Kemal Atatürk. This made the language easier to learn and fostered literacy.

Turkish Literature

Turkish literature

has a rich history divided into Old Turkish, Ottoman Turkish, and Modern Turkish periods.

Early Turkish Literature

Orkhon Inscriptions: Considered the first written texts of Turkic languages, these were carved in stone and reflect the history and philosophy of early Turkic peoples. Divan-u Lughat al-Turk: A 11th-century dictionary by Mahmud al-Kashgari, including early examples of Turkic poetry.

Ottoman Turkish Literature

During the Ottoman Empire, Turkish literature became more sophisticated. Influenced by Persian and Arabic traditions, it flourished in forms such as divan poetry, with themes of love, mysticism, and nature. Fuzuli: A notable poet known for his lyrical and romantic poetry. Evliya Çelebi: A famous travel writer who authored Seyahatname (Book of Travels).

Modern Turkish Literature

Modern Turkish literature, after the language reform in the 1920s, saw a revival of simpler language and themes reflecting modern life and national identity. Nazım Hikmet: A revolutionary poet and playwright. Orhan Pamuk: A contemporary novelist and Nobel laureate known for works like My Name is Red. Turkish Drama and Plays Theatre in Turkey gained prominence in the 19th century. Notable plays include: Namık Kemal's "Vatan Yahut Silistre": A nationalist drama. Works by Muhsin Ertuğrul, a pioneer of modern Turkish theatre.

Letters & Numbers

Turkish Alphabet and Numbers

The Turkish alphabet consists of 29 letters, based on the Latin script, with some additions to represent unique Turkish sounds.

Letters:

Vowels: 8 (a, e, ı, i, o, ö, u, ü) Consonants: 21 Special letters: ç, ğ, ı, ö, ş, ü

Numbers

Numbers in Turkish are written as digits (0–9) and are represented in the same way as Western numerals, though pronounced uniquely. Examples:
1 = bir
10 = on
100 = yüz
